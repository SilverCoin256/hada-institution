01Mission

Structure the evidence, not the hype.

As AI systems advise, augment, and replace human decision-makers, the evidence about their effects is fragmented across economics, medicine, human-computer interaction, organizational studies, and law — fields that rarely read one another.

The Atlas organizes that scattered literature by the decisions themselves rather than by the papers that study them, so that a student, a journalist, a researcher, or a policymaker can see at a glance what is established, what is disputed, and what remains unknown. Our commitment is not to be comprehensive but to be honest: to represent uncertainty faithfully, to keep contradicting studies on the page, and to update as understanding advances. Every claim answers three questions within one screen — What do we know? How sure are we? What don't we know?

03How the Atlas works

Five objects. Everything on this site is one of them.

The atomic unit of the Atlas is not a chart and not a citation. It is a structured claim with an evidence dossier and a confidence grade — situated among its contradictions and its gaps.

OBJ · 1

Claim

A neutral, falsifiable statement about what AI does to a specific human decision, in a specific context. Carries a permanent identifier.

OBJ · 2

Evidence dossier

Every study behind the claim: design, setting, population, effect, and what it does not show. No artifact, no number.

OBJ · 3

Confidence grade

Strength of evidence, computed against a published rubric — never editorial mood. Five levels, from Robust to Insufficient.

OBJ · 4

Contradiction

Where credible studies disagree, the disagreement is kept on the page — with suspected moderators named, not smoothed over.

OBJ · 5

Knowledge gap

What remains unknown, registered as a first-class object with its own identifier — so absence of evidence is visible and citable.

04Worked example · claim dossier

One claim, fully worked.

This is the founding dossier of the Atlas, shown in full. Every dossier follows this anatomy.

Domain 01 · AI-assisted judgment at work AI assistance compresses the performance gap between novice and expert workers — in short-run, task-level settings.

HADA·W-001 · v0.1 Moderate
Evidence on file4 studies
Brynjolfsson, Li & Raymond — Quarterly Journal of Economics, 2025
Staggered field deployment

5,000+ customer-support agents given a generative AI assistant. Average productivity rose ≈15%; gains were concentrated among the least-experienced agents, with minimal effect for the most experienced.

Noy & Zhang — Science, 2023
Preregistered RCT

453 professionals on incentivized writing tasks. ChatGPT cut task time ≈40% and raised quality ≈18%; the performance gap between weaker and stronger writers narrowed.

Dell'Acqua et al. — Harvard Business School WP 24-013, 2023
Field experiment

758 BCG consultants. On tasks inside the model's competence, below-median performers gained most (+43% vs +17% for top performers). On tasks beyond it, AI users were 19 percentage points less likely to produce correct solutions — see W-002.

Peng et al. — arXiv:2302.06590, 2023
RCT

95 developers, standardized programming task with GitHub Copilot: completion time fell 55.8%. Consistent with compression at the task level; does not measure skill formation.

Boundary conditions
  • Holds in short-run, task-level measurements — weeks to months, individual output.
  • Evidence is drawn from routine knowledge work: support, writing, consulting tasks, coding. High-stakes domains are untested (→ G-004).
  • Reverses at the frontier of model competence: on tasks beyond the model's ability, assistance degrades novice performance most (W-002).
  • Says nothing about long-run skill formation — whether compressed gaps reflect learning or dependence is unknown (→ G-001).
Contradiction on file
Adjacent claim W-004 is contested
A
Algorithm aversion. People abandon algorithmic advice after seeing it err, even when it outperforms them (Dietvorst, Simmons & Massey, JEP: General, 2015).
B
Algorithm appreciation. Lay judges weight identical advice more when told it comes from an algorithm (Logg, Minson & Moore, OBHDP, 2019).

Suspected moderators — expertise, task subjectivity, visibility of errors — are documented but unmapped. The Atlas records the disagreement; it does not vote.

What would change this grade
Registered uncertainty
  • Longitudinal field studies of skill formation under sustained assistance. First observational evidence of erosion exists — endoscopists' unassisted detection rates fell after months of AI exposure (Budzyń et al., Lancet Gastroenterology & Hepatology, 2025; single study, not yet replicated → W-006).
  • Replication in high-stakes, regulated decisions.
  • Organization-level productivity accounting, not task-level output.
Cite this claim
Suggested citation Human-AI Decision Atlas (2026). "AI assistance compresses the novice–expert performance gap in short-run, task-level settings." Claim HADA·W-001, v0.1, Domain 01.

06Confidence framework

Grades are computed against a rubric, not a mood.

Adapted from the GRADE tradition in evidence-based medicine. A grade can move only when the evidence moves — and every regrade is logged.

Robust
CriteriaThree or more independent studies across at least two designs, including randomized or quasi-experimental evidence; consistent direction; replicated in two or more contexts; no unresolved credible contradiction.
Current exampleW-005 — short-run, task-level output gains from generative AI assistance in routine knowledge work.
Moderate
CriteriaMultiple studies with a consistent core finding, but confined contexts, short horizons, or a design monoculture. Direction trusted; scope not yet.
Current exampleW-001 — novice–expert compression. Four studies, one consistent direction, all short-run.
Limited
CriteriaA single study or single setting. Direction plausible; magnitude unstable; replication absent.
Current exampleW-006 — post-exposure skill erosion. One observational study, not yet replicated.
Contested
CriteriaCredible studies point in opposite directions; moderators are suspected but unmapped. The disagreement is the finding.
Current exampleW-004 — advice-weighting instability: algorithm aversion vs. algorithm appreciation.
Insufficient
CriteriaThe question is posed; usable evidence does not exist. No grade is assigned — the entry moves to the gap register.
Current exampleG-001 — long-run skill formation under sustained AI assistance.

07Knowledge gap register

The blank spaces are the point.

A good map shows where the terrain is uncharted. Gaps are first-class objects here: registered, identified, and citable — so that "we don't know" can be said precisely, and research can aim at it.

G-001

Long-run skill formation under sustained AI assistance

Every study behind W-001 measures weeks or months. Whether compressed performance gaps reflect accelerated learning or accumulated dependence is unknown — and the two imply opposite policies.

What would resolve itLongitudinal field studies tracking unassisted performance after extended AI exposure. One early observational signal exists (W-006); it needs replication and design variety.

G-002

Tacit-knowledge transmission when AI absorbs entry-level work

Novices historically learn by doing the routine tasks AI now performs. Whether expertise pipelines thin out — and how fast — is unmeasured in any published study we have reviewed.

What would resolve itCohort studies of skill acquisition in occupations with and without heavy AI delegation of junior tasks.

G-003

Organizational outcomes versus task-level productivity

Nearly all causal evidence is individual and task-level. Whether task gains survive aggregation — through coordination costs, quality control, and workflow redesign — is an open question.

What would resolve itFirm-level accounting studies with credible identification, not extrapolation from task benchmarks.

G-004

Transfer to high-stakes, regulated decisions

The strongest experiments sit in low-stakes knowledge work. Medicine, sentencing, and credit carry accountability structures, liability, and time pressure that plausibly change how humans use AI advice.

What would resolve itField evidence from regulated settings with real consequences — and honest reporting when effects fail to transfer.

08Research methodology

Trust is a procedure, not a claim.

The Atlas has no institutional brand to borrow. Its substitute is radical methodological transparency: every editorial rule is published, and every rule is checkable against the pages themselves.

Number provenance is absolute

Every statistic on a claim page traces to a named, published artifact. No source, no number — without exception. Interpolations and schematics are labeled as such in the caption.

Grades follow a published rubric

Confidence grades are computed against the criteria above, written down before the evidence is assembled — not adjusted to fit a preferred conclusion.

No silent regrades

Claims are versioned. When new evidence moves a grade, the change is logged with its reason, and the prior version remains retrievable.

Dissent stays on the page

Contradicting studies are not smoothed into a false consensus. Where the literature disagrees, the disagreement is the content.

Description, not prescription

The Atlas reports what evidence says about AI and human decisions. It does not recommend policy. Argument belongs downstream, in the work of the people who cite it.

Unknowns are first-class

Every dossier ends with what would change its grade. Every domain carries a gap register. Honesty about the frontier is the product, not a disclaimer.

09Contributor model

Built to outgrow its founder.

For domain researchers

Domain reviewer

Check the dossiers in your subfield: missing studies, misread effects, wrong grades. Reviewer names appear on every dossier they verify — credit is the currency of this project.

For anyone

Correction

Any claim page can be challenged. Corrections are logged publicly with their resolution — accepted or declined, with reasons. A correction that improves a dossier is credited on it.

For authors

Study submission

Published a result that belongs in a dossier — supporting or contradicting? Submit it. Inclusion follows the rubric, not the conclusion.

Founding-phase disclosure. The Atlas is currently edited by one person — Shaurya Gupta, an independent researcher studying AI and institutions — and no dossier has yet passed external review. That fact is stated on every dossier until it changes. The design goal is an editorial network in which every claim is reviewed by named researchers in its field; the founding editor's job is to make himself replaceable.

The permanence rule: identifiers are never reused and never deleted. If a claim is retired — superseded, merged, or overturned — its identifier resolves permanently to the retirement notice and the full version history. A citation to the Atlas should never rot.

11Roadmap

Sequenced, not scheduled.

Each stage ships when it meets the methodology, not a date. Depth first buys the right to breadth later.

Full dossiers for the five remaining claims of AI-assisted judgment at work. The public correction channel opens; the first external reviewers are recruited.

v0.3

Candidate: AI in institutional review and oversight queues — the domain in which the founding research program works. Identifier scheme frozen; changelog becomes public.

v1.0

Every dossier carries at least one named external reviewer. Machine-readable exports of claims, grades, and gaps. At that point — and not before — the Atlas will call itself reviewed.

The measure of success is not traffic. It is the day a scholar, writing a sentence, reaches for a HADA identifier to support it.

HADA maps what humanity actually knows — and does not yet know — about how AI is changing the way people and institutions decide.
